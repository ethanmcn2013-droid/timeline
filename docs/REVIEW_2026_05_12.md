# Signal Timeline — product review · 2026-05-12

Creative-director read after Cycle 8 (viewer rebuilt at the GTM beat,
milestones added). One pass, three lenses: what holds, what leaks,
what to ship next.

This is partisan, not neutral. I push where I think the product is
weaker than the marketing claims. Where I push hardest is where I
think the product is closest to being great — and where drift would
cost the most.

---

## 1 · What holds — and why

**The viewer now reads as a working document, not a CMS dump.**
The Tasks GTM page works because every element is doing emotional
work: the date range names a window of accountability, the dial
implies a finish line, the T-N pills make the reader feel a calendar
moving. Importing that beat into Roadmap was the right call because
Roadmap's *job* is the same: turn "we're working on stuff" into "we
are accountable for this set of moments." The first paint of the
hero strip should now read as a commitment, not an inventory.

**Milestones as bold-in-markdown is a clean primitive.**
Schema already supported `kind = "milestone"` + `isLaunch`; only the
parser was lying about it. Wrapping a bullet in `**…**` to promote is
muscle-memory for anyone writing markdown — no new mental model. It
keeps the source-of-truth rule honest: markdown is canonical, the DB
is a parse cache. Anything that needed a UI-only override layer would
have leaked complexity into the editor and the cycle log.

**Per-milestone progress is honest progress, not vanity progress.**
"% done" can mean anything. Tying it to *dated items due on or before
the milestone* makes the dial measure the right thing: are we
shipping the work this milestone depends on, or are we pretending?
For un-dated workspaces it falls back to overall ratio — soft, but
not silently zero. Worth keeping the fallback documented in the help
text once the editor surfaces these mechanics.

**The chrome is still quiet.**
No coloured chip soup. No iconography clutter. Status reads through
the dial + numbers + tasteful row dots, which is the Tasks bar.

## 2 · What leaks

These are the places the product makes claims it can't fully cash.

### 2.1 — "Direction clarity" is the pitch, but a reader can't tell *why* a thing matters.

Items have title + description + status + date + project. A reader
looking at "Invite-only beta · 2026-05-18" can see *what* and *when*
but not *why this, why now*. The Tasks GTM page makes this implicit
because every row is in the context of "shipping toward Show HN
T-40." Roadmap items don't yet inherit that emotional context from
their nearest milestone. A milestone is a moment; a reader needs to
feel an item *belongs* to a milestone.

The cheap fix is rhetorical: when an item's targetDate is on or
before the next un-shipped milestone, render a soft "→ for Public
launch" line under it. The expensive fix is structural: let items
explicitly cite a milestone they belong to and group the list under
milestone headings on the viewer. I'd recommend the cheap fix first;
the structural one only after we see whether readers ask for it.

### 2.2 — The blocker concept is decorative.

Tasks GTM's blockers gate the rest of the work — "12 unresolved ·
gating roadmap progress" is a stake. Roadmap's blocker card just says
"blocked for N days." There's no `unblocks` count, no causality. So
the section *looks* like Tasks GTM but reads as a list of stuck items
with no consequence.

I'd refuse the temptation to ship an `unblocks` schema in v1.
Causality is real work — graph editing UX, cycle detection, cross-
project edges. Instead, I'd rename the section heading to match the
truth: "Held up" instead of "Blockers", and remove the *"gating
roadmap progress"* subtext I almost left in. The current copy says
"the moments the rest of this is building toward" for milestones;
keep that voice. Blockers should read "what we're stuck on" — flat,
honest, not loaded with implied causality.

### 2.3 — The dial isn't load-bearing for early-stage workspaces.

A 25% dial on a 9-item workspace with most items un-dated is
meaningless theatre. The dial only earns its prominence when the
workspace has weight — items in motion, dates, a credible cadence.
For new workspaces it should hide or downgrade to a sentence ("3
shipped, 6 to go") until the workspace has at least, say, 8 items
or one milestone.

This is the kind of thing that distinguishes a serious product from
a serious-looking one: knowing when to *not* show the dashboard
furniture. Worth adding a `hasMomentum` check that drives the
hero's right column.

### 2.4 — The right rail is doing two jobs and one of them weakly.

"Next 14 days" + "Milestones" + "Shared update" + "Refusals" is a lot
of cargo for a sticky column. The shared-update line in particular
("The short version for anyone who just needs the state of the work")
is product marketing copy living in a stakeholder surface. It feels
like a label on a button we wish existed. Either commit to the
shared-update artifact as a real, distinct shape worth navigating to —
or fold it into the milestone-pinned narrative on the main column.

Recommendation: drop "Shared update" from the right rail. If the
milestone view does its job, the shared update is the milestone strip.

### 2.5 — `studio. shipping log` is not a workspace name a stranger reads cleanly.

The demo workspace name is a designer in-joke. A first-time visitor
sees "STUDIO. SHIPPING LOG · 2026-05-08 → 2026-07-01 · 8 WEEKS" and
has to decode "studio" before they get to the actual roadmap. This
breaks the brand promise of "plain English."

For the demo workspace specifically, I'd push to rename it to
something a stranger can parse on first read — `Signal Timeline ·
example workspace`, or even just `Signal Timeline`. Keep the
shipping-log frame as the *description*, not the *name*. The
banner ("public demo workspace") is already doing the framing job.

### 2.6 — Dissent inside my own recommendation.

I added the "Next milestone · T-N" lockup beside the dial because it
makes the hero feel alive. It also adds clutter. There's a real case
for stripping it back to dial-only and letting the milestones section
below do that work — the hero would breathe more and the milestones
would earn their own scroll. I'm 60/40 in favor of keeping it; 40 of
that 60 is "Tasks GTM does it, parity matters" — which is a worse
reason than I'd like.

## 3 · What to ship next — three sharpenings

In priority order. Each is one cycle.

### 3.1 — Item ↔ milestone linkage (the cheap version).

When an item's targetDate falls on or before the next un-shipped
milestone, render a soft `→ Public launch` line under the item in
the main list. Makes the reader feel the gravitational pull of the
next moment without any schema work. ~150 LOC.

### 3.2 — `hasMomentum` gating on hero furniture.

Hide the dial + next-milestone lockup when the workspace has fewer
than 8 items or zero milestones. Replace with a single understated
sentence in the hero. The dial earns its place when the work earns
its weight. ~80 LOC.

### 3.3 — Rename the demo workspace + reclaim the description as frame.

`studio. shipping log` → `Signal Timeline · example workspace`. Move
the shipping-log line into the description field. Update the seed,
re-run against Turso prod, update Signal HQ data. ~30 LOC + 1 prod
seed run.

## 4 · What to refuse — three.

These will get suggested. They shouldn't ship.

### 4.1 — Comment threads on milestones.

Roadmap has a locked refusal on comment threading. A milestone is
a moment, not a forum. The shared update is the conversation
surface. Don't crack this.

### 4.2 — Auto-derived milestones from "biggest item this month."

LLM-suggested or rules-derived milestones break the bold-in-markdown
contract. Milestones are *declared*, not detected — that's the whole
point. The author is making a public claim about what matters; the
product's job is to render that claim faithfully, not to second-guess
it.

### 4.3 — A separate Milestones page.

A `/[workspaceSlug]/milestones` route would feel like progress and
would actually be regress: it splits the moment-of-truth (here is
what we're shipping toward) from the work-in-flight (here is what
we're doing). The viewer's main column should remain the one place
a reader needs to look. If milestones need their own page later,
that's a signal the main viewer is failing — fix the viewer.

## 5 · Brand refinement — typographic and rhythmic

Not visual decoration. Rhythm.

### 5.1 — The h1 should always end in a period.

`studio. shipping log.` already does it. So does Tasks GTM's "The
other roadmap." It's a small thing that does a lot of work — it
asserts that the title is a *statement*, not a label. Roadmap should
enforce this in `<WorkspaceHero>`: if the workspace name doesn't end
in a period, append one. Costs nothing, signals everything.

### 5.2 — Meta strip is the brand's heartbeat.

`STUDIO. SHIPPING LOG · 2026-05-08 → 2026-07-01 · 8 WEEKS · 2
MILESTONES` is the most brand-coherent line on the page. It does
what the suite copy promises (plain English, dates, calendar,
moments). Lean into this rhythm wherever else we have heroes — the
homepage hero, the project-detail hero, the refusals hero. Different
content, same time-shaped meta strip.

### 5.3 — Stat row should rest on values, not labels.

The current row is `9 TOTAL  2 DONE  3 DOING…` — big tabular numbers
in semantic colour. This is the right register. Resist the
temptation to add a sparkline or a delta-since-last-week. The Tasks
bar holds because we let the numbers do the work without
ornamentation.

### 5.4 — The dial colour is shipping-green but should arguably be brand-violet.

Right now the dial fills in `--status-shipped` (green). It reads
"complete" but it could read "Roadmap." Switching it to
`--brand` (violet) would make the dial a brand mark in motion —
every workspace's progress dial is a fingerprint of the product
identity. Worth A/B-testing inside one session.

### 5.5 — Numbers should always be tabular.

I tabular-num'd the new components but want to flag this as a rule:
every count, ratio, T-N, and date in Roadmap reads as data, not
prose. The Geist Mono fallback for numbers is sometimes used in
Signal Studio surfaces; Roadmap doesn't and shouldn't — Geist Sans
with `font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums` is the right register
because it reads as restrained, not technical.

## 6 · One thing I'd push back on if you asked me to add it

If you asked for a Gantt-style timeline view, I would refuse. A
Gantt chart is the surface a *project manager* uses to defend a
plan to their boss. Roadmap's audience is the inverse — the
people who need to *understand* the plan without having to defend
it. The dial + milestone strip + dated list is the right register
for that audience. A Gantt chart would import a job-to-be-done
that Signal Timeline explicitly refused at founding (`docs/PRODUCT.md`
locks: no team tier, no private workspaces, no public directory).

The strongest counter-argument I can construct: "stakeholders want
to see overlap and dependencies at a glance." That counter is real
but it's a request for a different product. Roadmap's job is
direction clarity, not dependency management. Naming the no.

End of memo. The three sharpenings above (3.1 → 3.3) are the
shortest path to making this cycle's beat actually pay rent. Pick
one, ship it next cycle.

## Decision log — appended after the memo

### Cycle 10 (2026-05-12) — hasMomentum threshold set at 5, kept after second-guess

The §3.2 sharpening shipped with `hasMomentum = totalForProgress >= 5
|| milestones.length > 0`. The number 5 was a guess and I caught
myself second-guessing it in the closing notes of the cycle.

Resolved as creative-director: keep 5.

The dial does one specific job — it makes the *story* visible. "3
of 9 shipped" is a story; it has a beginning, a middle, a far end.
"1 of 3 shipped" is just a count, and a count is what the stat row
already shows. 5 is the floor where the ratio starts telling a
story. Below 5, with no milestone, the workspace is a backlog — and
backlogs don't earn dials.

The milestone OR-branch is the safety net: any workspace declaring
a milestone is making a public claim about stakes, and stakes always
earn the dial regardless of item count. A 2-item workspace working
toward "Public launch" keeps its dial. Only workspaces that are
*both* small *and* have no declared milestone hide it.

If real-world signal contradicts this, the fix is one character in
`[workspaceSlug]/page.tsx`. Until then, the rule holds.

### Cycle 10 (2026-05-12) — §5.4 violet dial refused

Memo §5.4 floated switching the dial fill from `--status-shipped`
(green) to `--brand` (violet). Considered and refused.

Green is the universal data-color for progress; readers don't have
to learn it. Violet would make the dial a brand mark in motion — a
nice line — but it would also conflate brand-identification with
progress-communication. Each currently does one job well: the
wordmark + the brand-square in the header *say* "Roadmap"; the dial
*says* "progress." Collapsing them dilutes both.

Not every surface needs to be a brand mark. The dial doing its job
plainly is the brand mark.
